    Welcome to Understanding Website Analytics

    INTRO A Note Before You Begin

    Welcome back. If you have been working through this curriculum, you now have a website you can manage, social media profiles that represent your business well, and the content creation skills to keep them active. This lesson is about the next step: understanding whether any of it is working. Analytics are the tool that answers that question. They can feel intimidating at first because they involve numbers and unfamiliar terminology. But analytics are not about math. They are about listening to your customers without having to ask them anything directly.

    Every time someone visits your website, clicks a link, or watches your post, that activity gets recorded. Analytics tools turn that recorded data into readable reports. This lesson will teach you exactly what those reports mean, which numbers actually matter for a small business, and how to use them to make one small improvement per month.

    Background

    Why Analytics Matter for Your Business

    CONTEXT The Big Picture

    Running a business without looking at your analytics is like keeping a store open without ever counting how many people walked in. You might have a general feeling of how things are going, but you are missing information that could help you make better decisions every single week.

    61%
    Of small businesses say they do not regularly review their website performance data
    3x

    Free
    Google Analytics, the most powerful website data tool available, costs nothing
    15 min
    Is all it takes per month to run a basic analytics review and identify one improvement

    The goal of this lesson is not to turn you into a data analyst. It is to give you enough knowledge to ask and answer one simple question each month: compared to last month, is my online presence growing, holding steady, or declining? That one question, answered regularly, is more valuable than any advanced report.

    Analytics are most useful as a trend tool, not a snapshot. A single good or bad day tells you very little. A pattern over three or four months tells you something worth acting on.

    Step 1 of 7

    Log Into Your Analytics Dashboard

    STEP 01 Getting to your data for the first time

    Before you can read your data, you need to know where to find it. Depending on your website platform, you may use Google Analytics, your platform's built-in stats tool, or both. Here is how to access each one.

    Platform Where to Find Analytics What It Shows
    Google Analytics analytics.google.com - Sign in with your Google account and select your property. Full website traffic data: sessions, visitors, page views, time on page, bounce rate, and more.
    Squarespace Dashboard, then click Analytics in the left sidebar. Built-in traffic overview including visits, unique visitors, and popular pages.
    Wix Dashboard, then click Analytics and Reports in the left sidebar. Traffic overview, visitor behavior, and site performance summaries.
    WordPress If Google Analytics is connected, use analytics.google.com. Otherwise, check the Jetpack plugin stats under Dashboard, then Jetpack. Depends on setup, but generally covers page views, visitors, and referral sources.
    First time using Google Analytics? If your student team connected Google Analytics to your site during your project, you already have data waiting. If it was never connected, reach out to your Voice the Companies student team and ask them to set it up. It is a one-time process that takes about 15 minutes.
    • Google Analytics vs. platform stats: Google Analytics is more detailed and works across any platform. Built-in platform stats are simpler but are a perfectly good starting point for most small businesses.
    • Set your date range: When you first open any analytics tool, set the date range to the last 30 days. This gives you a current snapshot that is recent enough to be useful but wide enough to show a pattern.
    • Tip: Bookmark your analytics dashboard directly so you can return to it in one click each month during your review.
    Step 2 of 7

    Sessions and Visits

    STEP 02 How many times people visited your site

    Sessions, also called Visits on some platforms, is the most basic and important number in your analytics. It tells you how many times your website was opened and used within a given period. Understanding exactly what this number counts will help you read it accurately.

    Metric Definition
    Sessions / Visits
    What it counts

    A session is one continuous visit to your website. It starts when someone arrives on any page of your site and ends when they leave or are inactive for 30 minutes. If the same person visits your site twice in one day, that counts as two sessions.

    What to look for

    Compare your sessions month-over-month. Is the number going up, staying flat, or declining? A growing sessions number means more people are finding and returning to your site. A declining number is a prompt to investigate why.

    A new business with 80 sessions per month that grows to 120 sessions the next month is showing stronger momentum than an established business with 500 sessions per month that stays flat for six months in a row. Growth rate matters as much as the total number.

    Unhelpful Reading
    "I only got 45 sessions this month. That is a bad number." -- Sessions totals without context tell you very little.
    Useful Reading
    "I got 45 sessions this month versus 30 last month. That is a 50% increase. Something I did worked, and I want to figure out what."
    Step 3 of 7

    Unique Visitors

    STEP 03 How many individual people came to your site

    Sessions and unique visitors are related but different numbers, and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes new analytics users make. Once you understand the distinction, both numbers become far more useful.

    Metric Definition
    Unique Visitors
    What it counts

    Unique visitors counts the number of individual people who visited your site, regardless of how many times they visited. If one person visits your site five times in a month, they count as one unique visitor but five sessions.

    What to look for

    Compare unique visitors to sessions. If sessions are much higher than unique visitors, it means a smaller group of people is visiting repeatedly, which is a sign of strong loyalty. If sessions and unique visitors are nearly the same, most of your visitors are coming only once.

    Scenario Sessions Unique Visitors What It Tells You
    High repeat visits 200 60 A loyal core audience visits frequently. Good for community or service businesses.
    Mostly new visitors 200 185 Lots of new people are finding you, but few are returning. Focus on giving them a reason to come back.
    Balanced 200 110 A healthy mix of new discovery and returning interest. A common pattern for growing small businesses.
    A note on accuracy: Analytics tools track visitors using browser cookies, which means some visitors may be counted as new each time they clear their cookies or use a different device. Unique visitor numbers are always estimates, not exact counts. Use them as directional guidance rather than precise figures.
    Step 4 of 7

    Understanding Bounce Rate

    STEP 04 What it means when people leave quickly

    Bounce rate is one of the most misunderstood metrics in analytics. Many business owners see a high bounce rate and immediately worry something is broken. In many cases, a high bounce rate is normal or even expected. This step explains what bounce rate actually means and how to interpret it correctly.

    Metric Definition
    Bounce Rate
    What it counts

    Bounce rate is the percentage of visitors who land on a page of your site and leave without clicking on anything or visiting a second page. A 70% bounce rate means 70 out of 100 visitors left after viewing only the page they arrived on.

    General benchmarks

    For most small business websites, a bounce rate between 40% and 70% is considered typical. Above 80% on a key page like your homepage or Contact page is worth investigating. Below 30% is unusually low and often signals a tracking issue.

    ⚠ High Bounce Rate Is Not Always a Problem

    Context determines whether your bounce rate is a concern. A visitor who arrives on your Contact page, finds your phone number, and calls you immediately counts as a bounce in Google Analytics. They got exactly what they needed, but the tool recorded it as a failure. Always ask yourself what a visitor was likely trying to do on that specific page before deciding a bounce rate is too high.

    When to investigate a high bounce rate: If your homepage, Services page, or any page that is supposed to guide visitors deeper into your site has a bounce rate above 75%, that is a sign your message or layout may not be connecting. Consider updating the opening headline, adding a clearer call to action button, or asking your student team to review the page.
    Page Type High Bounce Rate Concern? Why
    Homepage Worth investigating above 75% Your homepage should draw people further in. A high bounce rate here suggests your message is not landing.
    Contact Page Often normal People come to get your phone number or address and leave immediately after finding it. That is a success.
    Blog or Article Page Often normal People read the article they came for and leave satisfied. Bounce rate on blog content is rarely meaningful.
    Services or Menu Page Worth investigating above 70% This page should lead to either a booking, a purchase, or a contact form. High bounces here may indicate a design or clarity issue.
    Step 5 of 7

    Average Time on Page

    STEP 05 Whether people are actually reading your content

    Average time on page tells you how long visitors typically spend on a specific page before leaving or navigating elsewhere. It is one of the clearest signals you have about whether your content is holding attention or whether people are skimming and moving on.

    Metric Definition
    Average Time on Page
    What it counts

    The average number of seconds or minutes visitors spend on a specific page. Google Analytics calculates this by measuring the time between a visitor's arrival on a page and their click to another page. Note: time on the last page before a visitor leaves the site is not counted, which means averages are often slightly underreported.

    What is considered good

    For a homepage or service page, an average time on page of 1 to 3 minutes is healthy. For a blog post or detailed information page, 3 to 5 minutes or more suggests people are genuinely reading. Under 30 seconds on any important page is a signal worth looking into.

    • Short time on page on key pages may mean your content is too dense, your text is hard to read on mobile, or visitors are not finding what they expected when they arrived.
    • Long time on page on information pages is a strong positive signal. It means your content is valuable and your visitors are engaged enough to keep reading.
    • Improve time on page by breaking long blocks of text into shorter paragraphs, adding subheadings so content is easy to scan, including a relevant image or two, and making sure your page loads quickly on mobile devices.
    • Ask your student team: If a page that should hold attention consistently shows under 30 seconds average, share that data with your student team and ask them to review the page design and copy together with you.

    Do not compare your time on page numbers to large company websites. A local bakery with an average of 90 seconds on their menu page and a steady month-over-month increase is performing well. Benchmark against your own previous months, not against businesses with entirely different content and audiences.

    Step 6 of 7

    Reach vs. Engagement on Social Media

    STEP 06 Understanding how people interact with your social media posts

    Social media platforms have their own built-in analytics tools, separate from your website analytics. The two most important metrics to understand there are Reach and Engagement. Knowing the difference between them helps you judge not just how many people saw your content, but how many people actually cared about it.

    Metric Definition
    Reach
    What it counts

    Reach is the number of individual accounts that saw your post, story, or reel at least once. It tells you how wide your content spread. A post with a reach of 400 means 400 different accounts had that post appear on their screen.

    Where to find it

    On Instagram: tap any post and select View Insights. On Facebook: scroll below any post and click See Insights. On Google Business: open your Business Profile and tap Performance.

    Metric Definition
    Engagement
    What it counts

    Engagement counts the number of people who interacted with your post in some way: a like, a comment, a share, a save, a link click, or a profile visit triggered by the post. It tells you how many people were interested enough to do something beyond simply seeing the content.

    Calculating engagement rate

    Divide your engagement number by your reach number and multiply by 100. For example, 40 engagements divided by 400 reach equals a 10% engagement rate. Anything above 3% to 5% is considered healthy for a small business account.

    Low Engagement Signal
    A product photo with reach of 300 and 2 likes. Engagement rate: 0.7%. The post was seen but did not connect. Try a different angle, a more personal caption, or a direct question to the audience. -- Consider adjusting your content approach for this post type.
    High Engagement Signal
    A behind-the-scenes video with reach of 150 and 22 likes, saves, and comments. Engagement rate: 14.7%. Even with lower reach, this post built real connection. Make more content like this.
    Saves are an underrated signal. On Instagram, a save means someone thought your content was valuable enough to return to later. Posts with high save counts often indicate your content is genuinely useful, which is one of the strongest signals the algorithm uses to decide whether to show your content to more people.
    Step 7 of 7

    Build a Monthly Review Habit

    STEP 07 15 minutes per month is enough to stay informed and keep improving

    Knowing what metrics mean is only half the work. The other half is actually looking at them consistently enough to notice trends. This step gives you a simple, repeatable monthly review routine that takes 15 minutes and produces one actionable insight every time.

    Min 1 to 3
    Open your analytics and set the date range to the last 30 days Log into Google Analytics or your platform's stats dashboard. Set the date range to the past calendar month. Write down this month's sessions and unique visitors in a simple notebook or spreadsheet.
    Min 4 to 6
    Compare to last month Change the date range to the previous calendar month and note those same numbers. Are sessions up or down? Are unique visitors growing? Write the comparison. You are looking for a direction, not a perfect explanation.
    Min 7 to 9
    Check bounce rate and time on page for your top two pages Find which pages received the most traffic and look at their bounce rate and average time on page. Flag anything that seems noticeably different from the previous month and jot it down.
    Min 10 to 12
    Open Instagram or Facebook Insights and find your top-performing post Look at which post in the past month had the highest engagement rate. Ask yourself what was different about that post compared to others: the format, the subject, the caption style, or the time it was posted.
    Min 13 to 15
    Write down one thing to try next month Based on what you saw, choose one small thing to do differently next month. It does not have to be big. Examples: post one more behind-the-scenes photo, update the headline on your Services page, add a call to action button to your homepage, or try posting at a different time of day.

    Consistency matters more than depth. A business owner who spends 15 minutes reviewing their analytics every single month will outlearn and outperform one who does a deep dive once every six months and forgets about it the rest of the year.

    Quick Metrics Glossary

    REF Every term used in this lesson, defined in plain language

    Analytics dashboards use a lot of terminology. Here is a plain-language definition of every key term covered in this guide, plus a few others you are likely to see in your own dashboard.

    Term Plain Language Definition Found In
    Sessions One continuous visit to your website. If the same person visits twice, that is two sessions. Google Analytics, Squarespace, Wix
    Unique Visitors The number of individual people who visited your site, regardless of how many times they visited. Google Analytics, Squarespace, Wix
    Page Views The total number of pages loaded across all visits. One visitor looking at three pages counts as three page views. Google Analytics, Squarespace
    Bounce Rate The percentage of visitors who left your site after viewing only one page without clicking anything else. Google Analytics
    Average Time on Page How long visitors typically spent on a specific page before leaving or clicking to a new page. Google Analytics
    Reach The number of individual accounts that saw a social media post at least once. Instagram Insights, Facebook Insights
    Engagement The number of interactions a post received: likes, comments, shares, saves, and link clicks. Instagram Insights, Facebook Insights
    Engagement Rate Engagement divided by reach, expressed as a percentage. Tells you what portion of people who saw a post actually interacted with it. Calculated manually
    Impressions The total number of times your content was displayed, including repeat views. One person seeing a post three times counts as three impressions but one reach. Instagram Insights, Facebook Insights
    Traffic Source Where your visitors came from: direct (typed your URL), organic search (Google), social media, or referral (a link on another site). Google Analytics
    Wrap-Up

    Key Takeaways

    REVIEW What to Remember From This Lesson

    You covered a lot of ground in this lesson. Here are the four ideas that matter most and the ones worth returning to whenever you sit down for your monthly review.

    01
    Sessions count visits. Unique visitors count individual people.

    These two numbers work together. Sessions tell you how active your site is. Unique visitors tell you how wide your reach is. Both matter, and neither one alone tells the full story.

    02
    A high bounce rate is not always a problem.

    Bounce rate depends entirely on what the page is meant to do. A Contact page with a high bounce rate usually means visitors found what they needed and left. Context determines whether a bounce rate is a concern.

    03
    Trending up over time matters more than any single day.

    One bad day, one slow week, or even one slow month rarely means anything on its own. What you are looking for is direction over time. Three months of steady growth is far more meaningful than one great afternoon.

    04
    Compare month-over-month, not day-to-day.

    Daily numbers are noisy and unreliable for drawing conclusions. Month-over-month comparisons smooth out the noise and reveal real patterns. Set your date range to 30 days and compare it to the previous 30 days every time you review.
