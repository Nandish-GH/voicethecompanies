    Welcome to Website Ownership Basics

    INTRO A Note Before You Begin

    Glad you are here. If websites have ever felt intimidating, you are not alone. The main thing to remember is simple: your website belongs to you. You are allowed to log in, click around, and make updates. This guide walks you through it in plain language so you can do that with confidence.

    At Voice the Companies, we want small business owners to feel in control of their online presence. This first lesson gives you practical basics you can use right away.

    Step 1 of 7

    Logging In

    STEP 01 Getting Into Your Dashboard

    Before you can do anything with your website, you need to know how to get in. Think of this like unlocking the back door to your business - once you know where the key is, it becomes second nature.

    Platform Login URL What to Do
    WordPress yourwebsite.com/wp-admin Type your website address followed by /wp-admin. Enter your username and password on the login screen.
    Squarespace account.squarespace.com Visit account.squarespace.com and sign in with your email and password. Your site will appear in your dashboard.
    Wix manage.wix.com Go to manage.wix.com and log in through your Wix account to access your site editor and settings.

    Bookmark your login page right now. Most business owners log in through their platform's main website, not through a link on their own site. Saving the bookmark takes ten seconds and will save you a lot of searching later.

    ▸ Pro tip from your VTC student team
    • Once you are logged in, you will see your dashboard - the control panel for your entire website. Do not worry if it looks complicated at first. The next step will walk you through exactly what everything means.
    • Nothing changes on your live site just from looking around the dashboard. You can explore freely without worrying about breaking anything.
    • If you forget your password, every platform has a "Forgot Password" link on the login page. It will send a reset link to the email address associated with your account.
    Step 2 of 7

    Hosting vs. Domain

    STEP 02 Understanding the Two Parts of Your Website

    Many business owners are surprised to learn that a website is actually two separate things working together. Understanding this distinction helps you manage your website confidently and avoid confusion when something needs to be renewed or updated.

    Your Domain Name
    What it is The address people type into their browser to find you - for example, yourbusiness.com. It is the name of your digital storefront.

    Think of it as Your business's street address. It tells people where to find you.

    Renewal Usually renewed once per year. If it expires, people cannot find your site.
    Your Hosting
    What it is The server - essentially a computer - where your website's files, images, and content are stored and delivered to visitors.

    Think of it as The physical building at your address. It is where everything actually lives.

    Renewal Usually renewed monthly or annually. If hosting lapses, your site goes offline.
    Important: Your domain and hosting may be through the same provider (like Squarespace or Wix, which bundle both) or through separate companies (common with WordPress). Check the original setup emails from your student team to confirm where yours are managed.
    • If your site suddenly goes offline, the first thing to check is whether your hosting or domain has expired. Log into the provider account and look for renewal notices.
    • Set calendar reminders for your renewal dates - both domain and hosting - so you are never caught off guard.
    • Key distinction: You can own a domain name without having hosting, but your site will not load for visitors. You need both working together for your site to be visible.
    Step 3 of 7

    Navigating the Dashboard

    STEP 03 Finding Your Way Around

    Every website platform has a dashboard, and while they each look a little different, they all contain the same core sections. Here is what to look for and what each area does.

    Dashboard Area What It Does Safe to Edit?
    Pages Manage the main pages of your site - Home, About, Contact, Services, etc. Yes
    Posts / Blog Add, edit, or delete articles, news updates, or any regularly updated content. Yes
    Media / Images Upload and manage all photos, graphics, and files used on your site. Yes
    Settings Site title, contact email, general preferences - safe for basic edits. With care
    Appearance / Design Controls your site's theme, fonts, and layout. Best left to your student team. Not recommended
    Plugins / Apps Add-on programs that extend your site's features. Changing settings here requires technical knowledge. No

    Spend ten minutes just clicking around your dashboard without editing anything. Get familiar with where things are. The more you explore, the less intimidating it becomes - and nothing breaks just from looking.

    ▸ Recommended by your VTC student team
    • Most platforms have a live preview option that lets you see how your site looks to visitors without publishing any changes. Look for a "Preview" button, usually in the top right corner.
    • The left-side menu (in WordPress) or top navigation bar (in Squarespace and Wix) is your main way to move between sections of the dashboard.
    • A useful shortcut: Bookmarking the Pages section of your dashboard directly will save you time when you need to make quick updates.
    Step 4 of 7

    Making a Simple Text Edit

    STEP 04 Updating Your Business Information

    One of the most common things a business owner needs to update is basic contact information - a new phone number, adjusted hours, or a change of address. Here is how to do it safely, step by step.

    1
    Go to Pages and find the right page
    Usually the Home, Contact, or About page

    Log into your dashboard and navigate to the Pages section. Click on the page that contains the information you want to change.

    2
    Click Edit to open the page editor
    Look for an Edit or Pencil icon

    Every platform has a way to enter edit mode. In WordPress it may say "Edit with Elementor" or just "Edit." In Squarespace and Wix, clicking directly on your page content often opens the editor automatically.

    3
    Click directly on the text and edit it
    Just like editing a Word document

    Find the text you want to change, click on it, and edit just like you would in any text document. Highlight the old text and type the new information.

    4
    Click Save or Publish when done
    Usually a blue or green button in the top right

    When you are done editing, always save your changes. Look for a Save, Update, or Publish button - it is almost always in the top right corner of the editor.

    5
    Check your live site to confirm the change
    Open a new browser tab

    After saving, visit your live website in a new browser tab and navigate to the page you edited. Confirm your change looks correct before closing the editor.

    Good to know: Editing text through the page editor does not affect your site's design, layout, or any technical settings. It is the safest kind of edit you can make. Small content changes never require a developer.
    Step 5 of 7

    Updating an Image

    STEP 05 Working With Your Media Library

    Your website has a media library where all uploaded photos and files are stored. Replacing or uploading an image is straightforward once you know where to look.

    1
    Go to Media or Images in the dashboard
    Found in the left sidebar or main navigation

    Navigate to the Media or Images section from your dashboard menu to see all files currently uploaded to your site.

    2
    Upload a new image
    Click Add New, Upload, or the + button

    To add a new photo, click the upload button and select the file from your computer or phone. JPG and PNG files work for most purposes.

    3
    Replace an image on a specific page
    Go to the page editor and click on the image

    To replace a photo that is already on a page, go into the page editor, click directly on the image, and look for a Replace Image or Change option in the panel that appears.

    4
    Name your files descriptively before uploading
    Good for organization and SEO

    Rename image files before uploading when you can. For example, storefront-summer-2024.jpg is far more useful than IMG_4823.jpg when you are looking for it later.

    • Keep file sizes reasonable. Very large image files (above 2–3 MB) can slow your website down noticeably. If a photo is very large, free tools like Squoosh.app let you reduce file size before uploading.
    • Common formats: JPG is best for photos. PNG is best for graphics with transparent backgrounds, such as logos.
    • Alt text matters: When you upload an image, there will usually be a field for "Alt Text." Add a short description of the image - this helps visually impaired visitors and improves your site's search ranking.
    Step 6 of 7

    What Not to Touch

    STEP 06 Staying Safe While Exploring

    Curiosity is a good thing, and exploring your dashboard will never break your site on its own. That said, there are a few areas that are best left alone unless you have specific guidance from your Voice the Companies student team or a technical professional.

    ⚠ Plugin or App Settings

    Plugins are small programs that add features to your site - contact forms, analytics, security tools, and more. Changing their settings without understanding what they do can break those features in ways that are difficult to undo.

    Safe alternative: If a form or feature on your site stops working, contact your student team before touching plugin settings.
    ⚠ Theme or Template Files

    Your theme controls the visual design of your entire site - layout, fonts, colors, and structure. Editing theme files directly requires knowledge of HTML and CSS. An accidental change here can alter your entire site's appearance at once.

    Safe alternative: If you want to change how your site looks, describe what you have in mind to your student team and let them handle the technical side.
    ⚠ Custom Code Areas (CSS / HTML / Code Injection)

    Any area labeled Custom CSS, HTML Editor, or Code Injection should only be edited by someone who understands what the code does. Mistakes in these areas can make parts of your site invisible or broken for visitors.

    Safe alternative: Leave anything that looks like code exactly as it is. If you see an error on your live site, take a screenshot and share it with your student team.
    ⚠ Domain or DNS Settings

    These settings control how your website address connects to your hosting server. An incorrect change here - even a small one - can take your site completely offline for hours or longer.

    Safe alternative: If you ever need to transfer your domain or change a DNS setting, always work with your student team or hosting provider's support line.
    ⚠ Deleting Admin Users or Accounts

    Removing an admin account - including your own - can lock you out of your site permanently. Always confirm with your student team before adding, editing, or removing any user accounts.

    Safe alternative: If you need to give or remove access for someone, ask your student team to create a separate user account with the appropriate permissions.
    When in doubt, pause. If you are ever unsure whether something is safe to change, take a screenshot of what you are looking at and reach out to your Voice the Companies student team before proceeding. There is never any shame in asking.
    Step 7 of 7

    Password Security

    STEP 07 Keeping Your Account Protected

    Your website account is a business asset, and protecting it is just as important as locking your front door at night. These habits are simple but make a real difference.

    1
    Use a strong, unique password
    Mix of letters, numbers, and a symbol

    A strong password includes uppercase and lowercase letters, at least one number, and at least one symbol. Avoid using your business name, your birthday, or the word "password" in any form.

    2
    Store credentials securely
    A password manager is the safest option
    Recommended Tools
    • Bitwarden - Free, easy to use, works on all devices
    • 1Password - Popular option with a clean interface
    • Apple Keychain - Built into iPhone and Mac, free to use

    Avoid writing passwords on sticky notes or storing them in an unprotected spreadsheet or notes app.

    3
    Change your password every 90 days
    Set a repeating reminder right now

    Open your phone's calendar app right now and set a repeating reminder every 90 days labeled "Change website password." This single habit significantly reduces your risk of being hacked.

    4
    Never share your login credentials directly
    Use platform user management instead

    If you need to give someone access to your website - a new employee, a contractor, or a VTC team member - never share your personal login. Use your platform's built-in user management to create a separate account for them with only the permissions they need.

    5
    Enable two-factor authentication (2FA)
    An extra layer of protection at login

    Two-factor authentication requires a second confirmation step - usually a code sent to your phone - when logging in from a new device. If your platform offers it, turn it on. It is one of the most effective ways to prevent unauthorized access.

    Wrap-Up

    Key Takeaways

    REVIEW What to Remember From This Lesson

    You covered a lot of ground in this lesson. Here are the four ideas that matter most - the ones worth returning to whenever you need a quick reminder.

    01
    Your website is yours. You have the right to edit it.

    Ownership means having access and using it. You do not need permission or a technical degree to log in, look around, and make changes to content that belongs to your business.

    02
    Most dashboards are built for non-technical users.

    Platforms like WordPress, Squarespace, and Wix are designed so that everyday business owners can manage their own content. The menus may look complex at first, but they become familiar quickly.

    03
    Small edits through the admin panel rarely break anything.

    Editing text, updating images, and navigating pages are all safe actions. The areas to be cautious about - plugins, theme files, and code - are clearly labeled, and you now know to leave those alone.

    04
    Backing up before big changes gives you a safety net.

    If you ever plan to make a significant change to your site, ask your Voice the Companies student team how to create a backup first. Most platforms offer this with one click, and it gives you complete peace of mind.
