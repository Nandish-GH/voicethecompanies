    Welcome to Digital Security and Maintenance

    INTRO A Note Before You Begin

    Welcome back. Over the past three lessons, you have built a strong digital foundation: you understand your website, you have a presence on social media, and you know how to create content consistently. Now it is time to protect all of that work. This lesson is about digital security and regular maintenance - the habits that keep your accounts safe, your website running, and your business protected from the kinds of threats that affect small businesses every day. None of this is complicated. It is mostly a matter of building a few simple habits, and this guide will walk you through each one.

    Security can feel like a scary topic, and we understand that. But we want to reframe it for you: digital security is not about being paranoid. It is about being prepared. The steps in this lesson are the same ones that professional IT teams recommend for businesses of every size, and most of them take less than five minutes to complete.

    Background

    Why Digital Security Matters for Your Business

    CONTEXT The Big Picture

    Small business owners often assume that hackers are only interested in large corporations. That assumption is exactly why small businesses are such common targets. They tend to have weaker security practices, less monitoring, and fewer resources to recover from an attack. Understanding the landscape helps you take the right precautions without overcomplicating things.

    43%
    Of all cyberattacks are aimed at small businesses
    60%
    Of small businesses close within six months of a major cyberattack
    81%
    Of data breaches involve weak or reused passwords
    95%
    Of cybersecurity breaches are caused by human error, not technical flaws

    Those last two statistics are the most important ones for you to hold onto. The vast majority of security problems come from things that are entirely within your control: weak passwords, clicking on phishing links, and software that has not been updated. This is genuinely good news, because it means that the steps in this lesson will protect you against the overwhelming majority of real-world threats.

    You do not need to become a cybersecurity expert. You need to build seven habits. That is all this lesson asks of you, and those seven habits will put you ahead of the majority of small businesses when it comes to digital safety.

    Step 1 of 7

    Update Your Passwords

    STEP 01 A unique password for every account is your single most important security habit

    The most common way that accounts get compromised is not through a sophisticated hack. It is through a data breach at another website. When a large website gets breached, attackers collect millions of username and password combinations and then try those same combinations on every other major platform. If you use the same password on multiple accounts, one breach can expose all of them at once.

    ✗   Weak Password Habits
    Using the same password on your email, Instagram, Facebook, and website dashboard. The password is your business name followed by the year you opened.

    Why this is risky: One breach of any platform that uses this password exposes every other account simultaneously. Business names and founding years are also extremely easy for attackers to guess.
    ✓   Strong Password Habits
    Every account has a different password. Each one is at least 12 characters and includes uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and at least one symbol. Passwords are stored in a password manager.

    Why this works: A breach of one platform exposes only that platform. No other accounts are at risk, because none of them share that password.
    RULES What Makes a Strong Password
    Rule Why It Matters Example
    At least 12 characters long Longer passwords take exponentially longer to crack with automated tools A 12-character password is millions of times harder to crack than an 8-character one
    Mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols Increases the number of possible combinations dramatically Tr33House!47kQ is far stronger than treehouse47
    No real words, names, or dates Attackers run dictionary attacks that try common words and personal details first Avoid your business name, your street, your birthday, or your pet's name
    Unique to each account Prevents one breach from compromising all your other accounts Use a password manager (Step 2) to keep track of dozens of unique passwords without memorizing them
    ACTION Which Accounts to Update First

    If updating all your passwords feels overwhelming, start with these accounts in priority order. These are the accounts that cause the most damage if compromised.

    • Your primary email account. This is the master key. Most password reset emails go here, which means anyone who accesses your email can reset every other account you own.
    • Your website dashboard. Your WordPress, Squarespace, or Wix admin password. An attacker with this access can delete your site, inject malware, or lock you out entirely.
    • Facebook Business Page and Instagram Business Account. Loss of these accounts is extremely difficult to reverse. Meta's account recovery process is notoriously slow and unreliable.
    • Google Business Profile. Access through your Google account. Update your Google account password and secure it with two-factor authentication immediately.
    • Any platform where customers can make payments. Financial and transaction platforms carry the highest risk. These accounts should have the strongest passwords and always use two-factor authentication.
    A helpful strategy: Use a passphrase rather than a random string of characters. A passphrase is a sequence of four or more unrelated words, such as "correct horse battery staple." This kind of password is both long and genuinely difficult to crack, while still being possible to type without too much frustration. Add a number and a symbol to meet most platform requirements: "correct!horse4battery."
    Step 2 of 7

    Use a Password Manager

    STEP 02 Stop trying to remember passwords - let a secure tool do it for you

    The most common objection to using unique passwords for every account is memory. Nobody can realistically memorize 20 different complex passwords. That is exactly what password managers are designed to solve. A password manager stores all of your passwords in an encrypted vault that is protected by a single master password. You only ever need to remember one password to access all the rest.

    🔒
    Bitwarden - Free and Highly Recommended
    bitwarden.com - free for individuals and small teams
    Why Bitwarden

    Bitwarden is open-source, independently audited, and free for individual use. It works on every device and every browser, and it syncs your passwords across your phone, tablet, and computer automatically. It also includes a built-in password generator that creates strong, unique passwords for you with one click.

    How to Get Started

    Go to bitwarden.com and create a free account. Install the browser extension on your computer and the app on your phone. As you log into your accounts over the coming week, Bitwarden will offer to save each password automatically. Within a few days, your vault will contain most of your important accounts without any manual entry required.

    🔒
    Your Phone's Built-In Password Manager
    iCloud Keychain on iPhone or Google Password Manager on Android - already on your phone
    iCloud Keychain (iPhone and Mac)

    iCloud Keychain is Apple's built-in password manager. It saves passwords automatically when you log in on your iPhone, iPad, or Mac, and syncs them across all of your Apple devices. It also alerts you if any of your saved passwords have appeared in a known data breach. Find it in Settings, then Passwords on your iPhone.

    Google Password Manager (Android and Chrome)

    Google Password Manager works similarly and is available on any Android phone or any device using the Chrome browser. It saves, syncs, and autofills your passwords, and it checks them against known breach databases. Access it at passwords.google.com or through your Chrome browser settings.

    ⚠ Never Store Passwords in a Text File, a Note App, or a Spreadsheet

    Saving passwords in a plain text file, your phone's Notes app, or a spreadsheet is one of the most common and dangerous habits we see. These files are not encrypted, and if your device is ever lost, stolen, or compromised, every password in that file is immediately visible to anyone who opens it. Use a dedicated password manager instead - they are specifically designed to keep this information encrypted and secure.

    Already doing this? That is okay. Start moving your passwords into a proper password manager today, one account at a time. Once an account is in the manager, delete it from the text file or spreadsheet. Aim to have everything migrated within two weeks.
    Step 3 of 7

    Enable Two-Factor Authentication

    STEP 03 Two-factor authentication stops most account takeover attempts cold

    Two-factor authentication - often called 2FA - adds a second verification step to your login process. Even if an attacker has your correct username and password, they still cannot access your account without the second factor: typically a code sent to your phone or generated by an app. This single setting dramatically reduces the risk of unauthorized account access.

    99.9%
    Of automated account attacks are blocked by two-factor authentication
    2FA
    Available on Instagram, Facebook, Google, and most website platforms
    Free
    Two-factor authentication costs nothing to set up on any platform
    5 min
    Time required to enable 2FA on each platform
    HOW TO Enabling 2FA on Each Platform
    Platform Where to Find It Recommended Method
    Instagram Settings and Privacy, then Accounts Center, then Password and Security, then Two-Factor Authentication Authentication app (more secure than SMS text message)
    Facebook Settings, then Security and Login, then Two-Factor Authentication Authentication app. Also enable login alerts while you are in this menu.
    Google Account myaccount.google.com, then Security, then 2-Step Verification Google Prompt on your phone or an authentication app
    WordPress Install a free plugin such as WP 2FA, then follow the setup wizard Authentication app such as Google Authenticator or Authy
    Squarespace Account, then Login and Security, then Two-Step Authentication Authentication app is available; SMS is also an option
    Wix Account Settings, then Security, then Two-Factor Authentication SMS or authentication app, both available
    TOOLS Free Authentication Apps

    An authentication app generates a time-sensitive six-digit code that changes every 30 seconds. It is more secure than receiving codes by SMS text message, because text messages can be intercepted or redirected. Any of the apps below work with most major platforms.

    • Google Authenticator. Simple, free, and widely supported. Available on both iPhone and Android. No account required to use it.
    • Authy. Free, backs up your codes to the cloud. The main advantage over Google Authenticator is that if you lose your phone, you can recover your codes on a new device without being locked out of all your accounts.
    • Microsoft Authenticator. Free and highly rated. Works with any platform that supports standard authentication apps, not just Microsoft products.
    Important: When you enable 2FA on any platform, you will be given a set of backup codes. These codes let you log in if you ever lose access to your phone. Print these codes immediately and store them somewhere safe and offline - in a filing cabinet, a locked drawer, or a fireproof box. Do not store them digitally on the same device.
    Step 4 of 7

    Recognize Phishing Attempts

    STEP 04 Learn to spot a fake email before you click anything

    Phishing is when an attacker sends you a fake email that looks like it comes from a trusted source - your bank, your website platform, Instagram, or a well-known service - in order to trick you into entering your username and password on a fake website they control. Once you do, they have your credentials and can immediately log into the real platform. Phishing is the most common method of account compromise for small businesses, and it works because the emails look convincing.

    SPOTTER Real vs. Phishing - Side by Side
    ⚠ Phishing Email - Red Flags
    Subject: URGENT - Your account has been suspended From: support@instagr-am-security.net

    Dear Customer,
    We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your account will be permanently deleted in 24 hours unless you verify your information immediately.

    Click here to verify your account now

    Red flags: fake domain, extreme urgency, threat of permanent deletion, vague greeting, pressure to click a link
    ✓ Legitimate Email - What to Look For
    Subject: Action required - unusual login detected From: security@mail.instagram.com

    Hi [Your Name],
    We noticed a login to your account from a new device. If this was you, no action is needed. If this was not you, please secure your account through our app or website.

    instagram.com/accounts/login

    Green flags: real domain, calm tone, gives you the choice to ignore it if it was you, directs you to the real site rather than a link
    CHECKLIST The Seven Warning Signs of a Phishing Email
    • Urgency and threats. Phrases like "Act now," "Your account will be deleted," or "You have 24 hours." Legitimate companies do not threaten you with permanent consequences for not clicking an email link immediately.
    • A mismatched or misspelled sender domain. The part after the @ symbol is where to look. Compare "support@instagram.com" (real) to "support@instagr-am.net" (fake). Attackers add hyphens, change letters, or use different extensions like .net instead of .com.
    • A generic greeting. "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Companies that have your account information know your name and use it.
    • A link that does not match what it claims. Hover your mouse over any link (do not click) to see the actual URL it points to. If it does not match the company's real domain, do not click it.
    • A request for your password or sensitive information. No legitimate company will ever ask for your password via email. Ever. If an email asks for this, it is a phishing attempt regardless of how official it looks.
    • Unexpected attachments. An attachment you did not request from a company you were not actively doing business with. Even PDF files can contain malware. When in doubt, do not open it.
    • Poor spelling or unusual formatting. Professional companies proofread their emails. Obvious grammatical errors, strange fonts, or inconsistent formatting are often signs that the email was created hastily by an attacker.
    ⚠ What to Do If You Suspect a Phishing Email

    Do not click any links, open any attachments, or reply to the email. If the email claims to be from a platform you use, go directly to that platform by typing its real URL into your browser (for example, instagram.com or wordpress.com) and log in from there. If there is a real issue with your account, you will see it when you log in normally. You can also forward the suspicious email to the platform's official phishing report address, which most major companies publish on their security help pages.

    Already clicked a link? Change your password for that account immediately, using a device you trust. Then enable two-factor authentication if you have not already. Contact your Voice the Companies student team for additional guidance on next steps.
    Step 5 of 7

    Check for and Apply Updates

    STEP 05 Outdated plugins are the most common way small business websites get hacked

    Website software, themes, and plugins release regular updates for two reasons: to add new features and to fix security vulnerabilities. When a vulnerability is discovered in a popular plugin, the developer releases a patch. But attackers also know about that vulnerability - and they actively scan the internet for websites still running the old, unpatched version. Keeping your software updated is the simplest and most effective way to prevent this kind of attack.

    💻
    Updating on WordPress
    The most update-intensive platform - check at least once per month
    Where to Find Updates

    Log into your WordPress dashboard at yourwebsite.com/wp-admin. Look for the Updates option in the left menu (it will show a red number badge if updates are available). This page lists any available updates for WordPress itself, your active theme, and all installed plugins.

    How to Apply Updates Safely

    Before applying updates, create a backup of your site (Step 6 covers this). Then select all available updates and click "Update All." After the updates are applied, visit your live site to make sure everything still looks and works correctly. Most updates apply without any visible change. If something breaks, your backup allows you to restore the previous version.

    Enabling Automatic Updates

    WordPress allows you to enable automatic updates for minor releases and security patches. Go to Dashboard, then Updates, and look for the option to "Enable automatic updates for all new versions of WordPress." For plugins, each plugin's listing in the Plugins menu has an "Enable auto-updates" link. Turn this on for all plugins you trust and use regularly.

    💻
    Updating on Squarespace and Wix
    These platforms handle most updates automatically on your behalf
    What You Still Need to Do

    Squarespace and Wix handle core software updates automatically, which is one of their advantages for less technical users. However, you should still log into your dashboard at least once a month to review any notifications, check that third-party integrations are still functioning correctly, and look for any alerts from the platform about your account or billing status. A dormant account you never check is still a security risk even on a managed platform.

    Our recommendation: Set a recurring reminder on the first Monday of every month to log into your website dashboard and check for updates. Even if no updates are available, the habit of logging in regularly means you will notice anything unusual about your site much sooner than if you only log in when there is a problem.
    Step 6 of 7

    Set Up Automatic Backups

    STEP 06 A backup is only useful if it is recent - automate it so you never have to think about it

    A website backup is a complete saved copy of your site - its pages, images, settings, and database - that can be used to restore your site to a working state if something goes wrong. Backups protect you against hacking, accidental deletion, failed updates, and hosting problems. The most important thing about backups is that they need to be recent. A backup from six months ago is much less useful than one from last week.

    💾
    WordPress - Install a Backup Plugin
    UpdraftPlus is free, highly rated, and straightforward to set up
    Installing UpdraftPlus

    In your WordPress dashboard, go to Plugins, then Add New, and search for "UpdraftPlus." Install and activate the free version. Once activated, go to Settings, then UpdraftPlus Backups. From here you can configure automatic backup schedules and choose where your backups are stored.

    Recommended Settings

    Set your files backup schedule to Weekly and your database backup schedule to Weekly. For remote storage, connect UpdraftPlus to a free Google Drive or Dropbox account. This stores your backups in a separate location from your website server, which is important because if your hosting server has a problem, backups stored on the same server may also be affected.

    Testing Your Backup

    After setting up UpdraftPlus, run a manual backup immediately and confirm that the backup files appear in your chosen remote storage location. Do not assume the backup is working - verify it. Check once a month that the most recent automatic backup completed successfully.

    💾
    Squarespace - Built-In Backup Options
    Squarespace provides automatic daily backups on paid plans

    Squarespace automatically backs up your site content on all paid plans. You can also export your site content manually at any time by going to Settings, then Advanced, then Import and Export Content. Export your content as an XML file and store it in a folder on your computer or Google Drive. Do this at least once per quarter as an additional precaution beyond Squarespace's built-in backups.

    💾
    Wix - Built-In Version History
    Wix saves versions of your site automatically as you edit it

    Wix automatically saves version history as you make changes to your site. You can access this through the Editor by clicking the Site menu and selecting Version History. From there you can restore any previous version of your site. Like Squarespace, we recommend also periodically exporting any critical content - blog posts, product listings, contact information - to a separate document stored outside of Wix as an additional layer of protection.

    ⚠ Backups Stored Only on Your Website Server Are Not True Backups

    A backup stored in the same place as your live website does not protect you if your hosting server experiences a failure, a fire, a flood, or a hack that affects the entire server. Always store at least one copy of your backup in a separate location: a cloud storage service like Google Drive or Dropbox, or an external hard drive that is not permanently connected to your computer.

    The 3-2-1 rule: Security professionals recommend keeping 3 copies of your data, on 2 different types of storage, with 1 copy stored off-site (such as in cloud storage). For a small business website, this means: one live site, one backup on your host or a plugin, and one backup in Google Drive or Dropbox.
    Step 7 of 7

    Audit Account Access

    STEP 07 Remove anyone who no longer needs access to your accounts

    Over time, business owners tend to accumulate a growing list of people who have access to their digital accounts: a former contractor, an old employee, a family member who helped set things up, a previous web developer. Each of those accounts represents a potential point of vulnerability. If a former collaborator's email gets compromised, that is one more way an attacker could reach your platforms. Auditing access every six months takes about 20 minutes and dramatically reduces this risk.

    AUDIT Where to Check and Remove Access
    Platform Where to Find User Management What to Look For
    WordPress Dashboard, then Users, then All Users Remove any users with Administrator or Editor roles who no longer work with you. Former contractors often remain in this list indefinitely.
    Facebook Business Page Meta Business Suite, then Settings, then People Review every person listed with page access. Remove anyone who no longer has a business reason to access the page.
    Instagram Business Account Managed through Meta Business Suite alongside Facebook Revoke any third-party app connections you no longer use. Go to Instagram Settings, then Security, then Apps and Websites.
    Google Business Profile business.google.com, then Managers Review all managers and owners. Remove anyone whose role with your business has ended.
    Squarespace or Wix Settings, then Contributors or Collaborators depending on your platform Remove any contributor accounts that belong to people who are no longer actively working on your site.
    RULE The Principle of Least Privilege

    When adding new people to your accounts, only give them the minimum level of access they actually need to do their job. This principle is called "least privilege," and it limits the damage that can occur if any one account is compromised.

    • A social media manager does not need Administrator access to your website. Give them only access to the social media platforms they manage.
    • A contractor updating a blog post does not need Administrator access. WordPress has an Editor role specifically for this. They can write and publish posts without being able to change site settings, install plugins, or access other accounts.
    • A Facebook Page admin does not need to be a co-owner of your Google Business Profile. Keep these accounts separate. Each person should only have access to the specific platform they need.
    • When a working relationship ends, remove access the same day. Do not wait until your next audit cycle. Access removal should happen immediately whenever someone stops working with your business.
    Summary

    Key Takeaways

    SUMMARY The Four Things That Matter Most
    01
    A unique password for every account is your single most important security habit.

    Password reuse is the primary reason that one breach turns into many. Use a password manager so you never have to choose between security and convenience. Generate a unique, strong password for every account you own and let the manager remember them all for you.

    02
    Two-factor authentication stops most account takeover attempts.

    Even a stolen password cannot get an attacker into an account protected by 2FA. Enable it on every platform that offers it, starting with your email, your website, and your social media accounts. Use an authentication app rather than SMS for the strongest protection available.

    03
    Outdated plugins are the most common way websites get hacked.

    Log into your website dashboard at least once a month and apply any available updates. For WordPress sites, enable automatic updates for plugins where possible. For managed platforms like Squarespace and Wix, check your notifications monthly even though core updates are handled automatically.

    04
    A backup is only useful if it is recent. Automate it.

    Manual backups are forgotten. Set up automatic weekly backups and store them in a separate location from your website server, such as Google Drive or Dropbox. Verify once a month that the most recent backup completed successfully. Do not wait until something goes wrong to find out your backup was not working.

    Ongoing Maintenance

    Security Checklist and Maintenance Calendar

    CALENDAR How Often to Do Each Security Task

    Digital security is not a one-time project. It is a set of recurring habits. This calendar tells you exactly how often to complete each task. Add these as recurring reminders in your phone calendar so they do not get forgotten.

    Monthly Tasks
    • Log into your website dashboard and apply any available updates
    • Verify that your most recent automatic backup completed successfully
    • Scan your social media accounts for unusual activity or unauthorized posts
    • Review any security alerts or notifications from your platforms
    • Check that all linked platforms are still connected and working correctly
    Quarterly Tasks
    • Change your primary email and website passwords
    • Review your password manager and update any passwords flagged as weak or reused
    • Export a manual backup of any platform that does not have automatic backups
    • Review your Google Business Profile for accuracy (hours, photos, contact info)
    • Check that two-factor authentication is still enabled on all key accounts
    Twice-Yearly Tasks
    • Audit account access on all platforms and remove anyone who no longer needs it
    • Review all third-party apps connected to your Instagram and Facebook accounts
    • Update your backup destination if your cloud storage account has changed
    • Review your 2FA backup codes and store a fresh set in a safe location
    • Walk through this lesson again with any new team members or collaborators

    REF Digital Security and Maintenance - Quick Reference

    Print this page and keep it near your workspace. Everything from this lesson, condensed into one reference sheet.

    Steps 1 and 2 - Passwords and Password Manager
    Strong Password Rules
    • At least 12 characters
    • Mix of upper, lower, numbers, symbols
    • No real words, names, or dates
    • Unique to every single account
    Password Manager Options
    • Bitwarden (free, bitwarden.com)
    • iCloud Keychain (iPhone / Mac, built-in)
    • Google Password Manager (Android / Chrome)
    Never store passwords in text files, notes, or spreadsheets.
    Step 3 - Two-Factor Authentication
    Where to Enable 2FA
    • Primary email (most critical)
    • Website dashboard
    • Facebook and Instagram (via Accounts Center)
    • Google account (myaccount.google.com)
    Free Authenticator Apps
    • Google Authenticator
    • Authy (backs up codes to cloud)
    • Microsoft Authenticator
    Print and store backup codes offline in a safe location.
    Step 4 - Phishing Warning Signs
    7 Red Flags
    • Urgency and threats ("Act now," "24 hours")
    • Misspelled or mismatched sender domain
    • Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
    • Suspicious link URL (hover before clicking)
    • Request for your password via email
    • Unexpected attachments
    • Poor spelling or unusual formatting
    If You Suspect Phishing
    • Do not click any links or open attachments
    • Go directly to the platform by typing the URL yourself
    • If you clicked: change your password immediately
    • Contact your VTC student team for guidance
    Steps 5, 6, and 7 - Updates, Backups, and Access
    Monthly Update Habit
    Log into your website dashboard on the first Monday of every month. Apply all available updates for WordPress core, plugins, and themes. Visit your live site afterward to confirm everything works correctly.
    Backup Setup
    • WordPress: UpdraftPlus plugin (free)
    • Squarespace: built-in plus manual export quarterly
    • Wix: Version History plus manual export quarterly
    • Store backups in Google Drive or Dropbox
    • Verify backup completion once per month
    Maintenance Calendar Summary
    Monthly: check updates, verify backup, review notifications
    Quarterly: change key passwords, review password manager, manual backup export
    Twice yearly: audit account access, review 2FA codes
    Access Audit Locations
    • WordPress: Dashboard, Users, All Users
    • Facebook: Meta Business Suite, Settings, People
    • Google Business: business.google.com, Managers
    • Squarespace or Wix: Settings, Contributors
